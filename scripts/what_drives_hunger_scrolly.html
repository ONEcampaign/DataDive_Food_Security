Households in low and lower middle-income countries spend a much larger share of their income on food.

When the cost of food increases, the burden falls disproportionately on poor people who must use a larger share of their income to buy food.

Some may not be able to afford it.

Global food prices have been increasing since the start of the pandemic.

Russia's war in Ukraine has only increase the uncertainty in global food and commodity markets.

The prices for oils and cereals have increased significantly.

Since the diets of the poorest people tend to be dominated by staples such as wheat and maize (rather than meat) they are more vulnerable to these shocks.

Prices for wheat and maize, which are staples in many countries, have more than doubled since the start of the pandemic.

Prices for sunflower oil and palm oil, used for cooking in several countries, have also doubled.

But policies are making the situation worse.

In response to the Ukraine Invasion, 16 countries (up from 3 countries) are imposing export restrictions on food as of early April 2022. This represents 17% of total calories traded.

During the 2008 food crisis, these policies accounted for 40% of price increases.
